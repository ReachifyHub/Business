# Foundations

Read this before core-principles.md. These are the underlying principles — core-principles.md and each offer folder are applications of these, not separate rules.

## The product comes first
Marketing amplifies. A great product with average marketing still sells; average marketing pointed at a bad product just helps people find out faster. Automation and any digital product must actually work and actually help before a single email goes out. Never build the pitch first and rush the product to match it.

## Sell the feeling, not the product
Nobody buys "WhatsApp automation." They buy never losing a customer to silence again — the relief of knowing messages get answered whether they're free or not. Nobody buys "an ebook." They buy the specific outcome finishing it gets them. Before writing any pitch, ask: what does this person feel the moment this actually works for them? Sell that, not the mechanism.

## The "so what?" test for finding the real benefit
Push past the surface feature by asking "so what?" repeatedly until there's nowhere left to go. "WhatsApp bot that replies instantly." So what? Customers get answered even when I can't. So what? I stop losing bookings to whoever replies first. So what? I keep more of the revenue I'm already generating. That last line is the actual headline material — not the first one.

## First sale cheap, real money after
The hardest sale is the first one from a stranger. Where possible, lower the barrier to a small first yes rather than asking for the full commitment cold. For the automation offer, this may mean a smaller trial/setup step before the full monthly commitment. For any digital product (ebook, templates), a genuinely cheap entry point earns a buyer relationship worth more than the entry price itself.

## Give away the knowledge, sell the product
Free, genuinely useful content earns trust and builds the list. This applies to content *about* the offer (tips, insight, useful information) — not the product itself. A service or software isn't given away; the thinking and value around it is.

## Own the list — Telegram, Pinterest, and Medium are rented land
Any audience built on a platform can vanish overnight if the account is restricted. The actual asset being built through outreach is the list of real businesses and their contact details — not the reach on any one channel. Every campaign's underlying goal, beyond the immediate ask, is growing an owned list Mike controls directly.

## Know exactly who you're talking to
Every piece of copy should be written for one specific type of person, not "small businesses" generally — this is already reflected in the trades/salons split in audience.md, and should stay that granular as new offers are added.

## Copy over polish
Plain, direct writing consistently outperforms polished, corporate-sounding copy. This is already the house tone (see cold-email-structure.md and tone.md) — this principle is *why* that tone was chosen, not just a style preference.

## The headline/subject line is the whole game
Whatever gets attention first — subject line, opening sentence, ad headline — determines whether anything else gets read. Disproportionate effort goes here relative to the rest of any piece of copy.

## A generous guarantee lowers risk and raises conversion
Confidence in the product, expressed as a real guarantee, removes the buyer's biggest objection before they raise it. The automation offer currently has no stated guarantee — worth adding.

## Ads are for list-building, not closing — relevant once budget exists
When ad spend resumes, the ad's job is the opt-in/lead, not the direct sale of the core offer. Point ads at something low-friction (a demo, a lead magnet), not straight at the £180 setup fee.

## The email list is a bank account
Pure-value sends are deposits; asks are withdrawals. Anything sent to an existing list or contact should track which type it is, and heavy withdrawal-only sending burns the relationship.
