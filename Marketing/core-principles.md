# Core Principles

These apply no matter which offer is being pitched.

## The reader's situation

A UK small business owner gets dozens of cold pitches a week — most from agencies, most generic, most promising vague "growth." They skim in under 5 seconds. The subject line and first line decide if the email gets read past that.

## What makes an email survive the skim

- **Specific over generic.** "I noticed [Business] doesn't list evening bookings on your site" beats "I help businesses like yours grow."
- **One problem, one solution, one ask.** Not three services, not a feature list. The reader should finish the email knowing exactly one thing I do and exactly one next step.
- **Proof, not adjectives.** Don't say "powerful" or "cutting-edge." Show or link something real — a demo, a working example — and let it speak.
- **Short.** Under 150 words for a first-touch cold email. If it needs scrolling, it's too long.
- **No hype language.** Never: "revolutionary," "game-changing," "leverage," "synergy," "unlock," "supercharge," exclamation marks. Write like someone explaining a useful tool to a busy person, not selling a course.

## The ask

The only ask in a first email is a reply or a 2-minute look at a demo link. Never ask for a call, a meeting, or a deposit in email one. Lower the barrier as far as it goes.

## Personalization depth

Minimum bar before an email goes out: the business name, their sector-specific pain point, and one detail pulled from their actual website (what they offer, their hours, something they're missing). If none of that is available, the business gets skipped for that day rather than sent a templated email — a bad personalized-looking email that's actually generic is worse than not sending.

## Tone

Semi-formal. Full sentences, correctly punctuated, no slang, no forced casualness. But not stiff corporate voice either — write like a competent person who respects the reader's time, not like a legal disclaimer. Contractions are fine (I'm, don't, it's). Never use exclamation marks. Never overclaim what hasn't been proven yet.
