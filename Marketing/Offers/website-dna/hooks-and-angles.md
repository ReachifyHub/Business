# Website DNA™ — Hooks & Angles

## 1. The "It Doesn't Feel Like Us" Angle

The specific moment every AI-web-developer dreads: the client looks at the finished site, nods politely, and says those five words. It's not about bugs or typos — it's about the site being technically fine but spiritually wrong.

**Headline form:** "It looks nice, but it doesn't feel like us" — and how to never hear it again
**Paragraph form:** Every developer who builds with AI knows this moment. The site is clean, the layout works, the copy reads well — and the client says "it doesn't feel like us." Not wrong. Just not theirs. That gap isn't a prompting problem. It's a context problem. You gave AI instructions, but you didn't give it understanding.

## 2. The Two Developers Angle

Two developers use the same AI tool for the same type of client. One gets "nice but generic." The other gets "this is exactly what we wanted." The difference isn't the tool — it's what happened before the tool was opened.

**Headline form:** Same AI tool. Same client. Completely different website. Here's why.
**Paragraph form:** Two freelancers land the same type of project. Both use Cursor. Both ship in a week. One gets revision after revision. The other gets sign-off on the first draft. The difference? One started by understanding the business. The other started by opening the tool. The tool isn't the variable — what you bring to it is.

## 3. The Guessing Game Angle

Prompting AI without business context is guessing. You're hoping the right words appear, but you don't actually know what "right" means for this specific business. The book replaces guessing with a systematic understanding of what to ask and why.

**Headline form:** You're not prompting AI — you're guessing at it
**Paragraph form:** "Build me a website for a bakery" is a guess. You don't know what kind of bakery, who their customers are, what makes them different from the bakery across town, or why people choose them. So AI guesses too — and produces something that looks like every bakery site on the internet. The alternative isn't a better prompt. It's better context.

## 4. The Replaceable Developer Angle

If your only skill is "I can use AI to build websites fast," you're competing with everyone else who can do the same thing — and they're charging less. The strategist who understands businesses before building isn't competing on speed. They're competing on outcomes, and they charge accordingly.

**Headline form:** If AI can replace what you do, you're charging for the wrong thing
**Paragraph form:** When a client can go to Lovable or v0 themselves and get a decent site in 20 minutes, what are they paying you for? If the answer is "I use the tools better," that's a fragile position. The developer who gets paid more isn't the one who prompts faster — it's the one who asks the questions the client didn't know to ask, then feeds that understanding into the build.

## 5. The Beautiful But Generic Angle

AI is excellent at producing polished, professional-looking output. That's the problem. Every AI website is polished and professional — which means "polished and professional" is no longer a differentiator. The sites that stand out are the ones where something specific and human comes through.

**Headline form:** AI made every website beautiful. That's the problem.
**Paragraph form:** Five years ago, a clean layout and good typography were enough to impress. Now AI handles that by default. The baseline went up, but so did the sameness. Scroll through any portfolio of AI-built sites and they blur together — same hero sections, same "we empower" copy, same gradient accents. Beauty is no longer the differentiator. Specificity is.

## 6. The Prompt List Isn't Enough Angle

There's no shortage of "50 AI prompts for better websites." They help with phrasing. They don't help with understanding. A prompt is only as good as the context you bring to it, and a list of prompts doesn't give you context — it gives you templates.

**Headline form:** 50 prompts won't save you (and neither will 500)
**Paragraph form:** A better prompt for the same empty context just produces a more articulate guess. "Write compelling homepage copy for a fitness brand" is still a guess unless you know what makes this fitness brand different from the 400 others in the city. Prompt lists treat the symptom. The disease is showing up to AI with nothing to say.

## 7. The Portfolio Sameness Angle

If every site in your portfolio could have been built for any client in that industry, your portfolio is selling "I use AI well" instead of "I build what this specific business needs." Buyers notice.

**Headline form:** If all your AI websites look like they could belong to any client, you have a portfolio problem
**Paragraph form:** Open your portfolio. If the restaurant site could be for any restaurant, the coaching site could be for any coach, and the SaaS site could be for any SaaS — you've built a showcase of AI capability, not a showcase of client understanding. Clients who see generic work assume generic process. Clients who see specificity assume you'll bring that same care to their project.

## 8. The Discovery Before Delivery Angle

Most developers skip the part where they learn about the business because it doesn't feel like "real work" — it feels like conversation. But that conversation is the difference between a website that represents a business and a website that just occupies a URL. Doing the homework first isn't slower; it's faster, because the build needs fewer revisions.

**Headline form:** The fastest way to build a website has nothing to do with AI
**Paragraph form:** The fastest build isn't the one that starts with the fastest tool. It's the one that ends with the fewest revisions. And the number one source of revisions is a mismatch between what the client expected and what got built. That mismatch comes from skipping discovery. Not the formal kind with wireframes and approvals — the kind where you sit with a business owner and actually learn what they do before you start building.
