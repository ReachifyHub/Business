# Website DNA™ — Objections & Responses

## "I can just prompt better myself"

You can — for a while. Prompting is a real skill and improving it matters. But "prompting better" without business context is like writing better search queries for information you don't have. The issue isn't how you ask AI for something — it's whether you know what to ask for. This book teaches you how to figure out what "right" looks like for each specific client before you ever write a prompt. Better prompting on top of real understanding is powerful. Better prompting on top of nothing is just more confident guessing.

## "Is a $5–10 ebook actually going to help me?"

Fair question. Here's what $5–10 gets you: a structured process for understanding any business before you build, worksheets you can reuse with every client, and a prompt library that works because it's built on the context you've gathered. If one client approves your first draft instead of sending you through three rounds of revisions, the book paid for itself in the time you saved. If you can raise your rate even slightly because your work stops looking like every other AI build, it paid for itself many times over. The price is low because the product is early — not because the content is thin.

## "I don't have time to learn a whole system"

You already spend time on revisions, re-dos, and the back-and-forth of clients who can't articulate what's wrong but know something is off. That time adds up — often more than the hour it takes to read this book and run through the canvases with a client. The system isn't extra work; it replaces the disorganized, ad-hoc discovery you're probably already doing (or skipping, and paying for later in revisions). Think of it as front-loading the work so the build goes smoother, not adding work on top of it.

## "I already ask my clients questions before building"

Good — you're already doing some of this intuitively. The question is whether your process is consistent and complete, or whether it varies by client and mood. Most developers ask about colors, features, and "what websites do you like?" — which tells AI what to copy, not what to create. This book gives you a repeatable framework that covers the layers most people miss: how the business makes money, who the real customers are, what the brand voice actually sounds like. If your current questions already cover all of that systematically, you don't need this book. Most don't.

## "Won't AI just get better at this on its own?"

AI will get better at generating polished output. That's exactly the problem — "polished" isn't the gap. The gap is between polished and specific. AI doesn't sit across from a business owner and hear them talk about why they started. It doesn't notice the words they use when they describe their best customers. It doesn't pick up on the difference between what a client says they want and what they actually need. Understanding a business is a human task. The book teaches you how to do that understanding work and hand it to AI in a form it can actually use.

## "I use Framer/Webflow, not AI code tools — does this apply?"

Yes. The book isn't about AI coding tools specifically — it's about understanding a business before you build, regardless of what you build with. If you're using Framer or Webflow, you still face the blank-canvas problem. You still default to templates. You still get clients saying "it doesn't feel like us." The 7-layer system and canvases work the same way whether your output is AI-generated code or a no-code build — the context-gathering step is identical. Chapter 6 is the only chapter that's specifically about AI tools, and the principle there (structured context in, specific output out) applies to any tool.
