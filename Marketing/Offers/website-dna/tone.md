# Website DNA™ — Tone & Voice

## Core Voice: Peer, Not Guru

You are someone who builds websites with AI and got tired of the generic results. You figured out a system that works. You're sharing it with other people who have the same problem — not lecturing from a stage, not selling a lifestyle, not promising transformation. Just saying "here's what I learned about why the output was bad and what fixed it."

## Derived Rules (from house writing rules, applied to this product)

### Sell the feeling, not the product
The feeling is: the moment a client looks at your work and says "this is exactly us" instead of "it's nice but..." That's the payoff. Don't sell "a 7-chapter system" — sell the specific relief of never hearing "it doesn't feel like us" again. Push past the surface: "You'll build better websites" → so what? → "Your clients will actually approve the first draft" → so what? → "You'll stop being the person who makes pretty things and start being the person who makes right things." Land on that last one.

### Plain and specific beats polished
Write like you're explaining it to a colleague over chat. "The client said the homepage felt like it could belong to anyone" is better than "AI-generated websites often lack brand differentiation." Specific moments, specific language. No: "game-changing," "revolutionary," "unlock," "supercharge," "next-level," "ultimate," or exclamation marks that haven't earned their place.

### The headline is the whole game
Every piece of promotional content lives or dies by its first line. The headline should make one specific person think "that's exactly my problem" — not describe what the book contains. "Build Websites That Don't Look AI-Generated" works because it names the exact embarrassment. "A Comprehensive Guide to AI Website Strategy" doesn't work because it describes the product, not the pain.

### Give away real value
The Medium article, the Quora answer, the Pinterest tip — each one should teach something genuine that works even if the reader never buys the book. A reader who walks away with a better understanding of why their AI websites look generic has gotten something real. That earns trust. The book is for people who want the full system with worksheets, not just the free sample.

### No invented credibility
This is a new product. Don't say "1000+ developers trust this system" or "the #1 ebook for AI web developers." The credibility is in the system itself: it's structured, it's specific, and it addresses a real problem with a real process. If you wouldn't say it to a friend who asked "so is this actually good?", don't write it in copy.

### One call to action per piece
Don't end with "buy the book, subscribe to my newsletter, follow me on Twitter, and leave a comment." One ask, clearly placed, after the value has been delivered.

## Language Patterns to Use

- "You know that moment when..." (naming shared experiences)
- "The problem isn't X — it's Y" (reframing)
- "Here's what I found..." (discovery, not authority)
- "Try this:" (practical, immediate)
- Questions the reader would actually ask themselves
- Short declarative sentences after longer explanatory ones (creates rhythm and emphasis)

## Language Patterns to Avoid

- "Imagine a world where..." (doesn't name the real problem)
- "In today's fast-paced..." (filler, says nothing)
- "Let me show you how to 10x..." (overpromising)
- Any sentence that could appear unchanged on a SaaS landing page
- Language that positions the writer as a coach/expert/guru rather than a practitioner
- Rhetorical questions where the answer is obviously "yes" (they feel manipulative)
